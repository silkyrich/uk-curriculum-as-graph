## Section 1: Free Trade and Comparative Advantage

**Section ID:** `S01` | **Est. time:** 15 min

**Spec points:** `3.2.6.2-comparative-advantage`, `3.2.6.2-absolute-advantage`, `3.2.6.2-benefits-of-trade`

### Learning Objectives

- [AO1] Distinguish comparative advantage from absolute advantage
- [AO2] Explain the gains from trade using specialisation and opportunity cost
- [AO3] Identify the political economy reason why protectionism persists despite economic consensus for free trade

### Concepts

**Introduced this section:**
- **Comparative Advantage** (`C001`): The ability of a country to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country, providing the basis for mutually beneficial trade even if one country is absolutely more efficient at producing everything.
- **Absolute Advantage** (`C002`): The ability of a country to produce more of a good than another country using the same quantity of resources. Distinct from comparative advantage — absolute advantage does not determine the pattern of trade.
- **Opportunity Cost** (`C003`): The value of the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made. The basis for calculating comparative advantage: the opportunity cost of producing one good expressed in units of the other good not produced.
- **Free Trade** (`C004`): International trade conducted without government-imposed restrictions such as tariffs, quotas, or export subsidies. Under free trade, the domestic price of traded goods equals the world price.

### Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should have covered comparative advantage in an earlier session. Do not re-teach — use this section as a 5-minute mental reset to establish the free trade baseline against which protectionism is measured.

### Teaching Sequence

Hook slide (Trump tariffs anecdote, 2 min) → learning objectives contract with class → section header recap → free trade baseline content slide (5 min including political economy point)

### Timing

Hook: 2 min | Instruction: 8 min | Guided Practice: 3 min | Independent: 2 min | **Total: 15 min**

### Misconceptions to Pre-empt

- **Error:** Students conflate absolute advantage with comparative advantage — 'Country A is better at everything, so it should produce everything.'
  **Correction:** Comparative advantage depends on relative opportunity costs, not absolute productivity. Both countries gain from trade even when one is absolutely more efficient in all goods. Use a verbal example before the numerical one. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2022)*

### Board Work

Write on board and leave visible: 'Comparative advantage = lower OPPORTUNITY COST, not higher absolute output.' Return to this when students make the absolute/comparative confusion.

### Differentiation

**Stretch/A*:** Ask: if comparative advantage guarantees gains from trade, why do governments not simply remove all barriers? The political economy of concentrated losers and diffuse winners.

**Support:** Use a simple two-country, two-good table on the board. Have students calculate opportunity costs before explaining the concept.

## Section 2: Instruments of Protectionism — Tariffs

**Section ID:** `S02` | **Est. time:** 25 min

**Spec points:** `3.2.6.2-tariffs`

### Learning Objectives

- [AO1] Define tariff (ad valorem and specific) and explain its mechanism
- [AO2] Draw and fully label a tariff diagram with all welfare areas
- [AO3] Explain the welfare effects of a tariff using areas A, B, C, and D

### Concepts

**Introduced this section:**
- **Tariff** (`C005`): A tax imposed on imported goods at point of entry, raising the domestic price above the world price. May be ad valorem (percentage of import value) or specific (fixed amount per unit).
- **Consumer Surplus** (`C006`): The difference between the maximum price a consumer is willing to pay for a good and the price they actually pay. Represented on a supply-and-demand diagram as the area below the demand curve and above the market price.
- **Producer Surplus** (`C007`): The difference between the price a producer receives for a good and the minimum price they would have accepted. Represented on a diagram as the area above the supply curve and below the market price.
- **Deadweight Loss** (`C008`): The loss of economic efficiency that occurs when the equilibrium outcome is not achieved. In the tariff diagram, represented by triangles B and D — welfare destroyed by production inefficiency and consumption reduction that is transferred to no party.

**Reinforced from earlier sections:**
- Comparative Advantage (`C001`)
- Opportunity Cost (`C003`)
- Free Trade (`C004`)

### Prerequisite Knowledge

Students must have solid knowledge of supply and demand, and consumer/producer surplus from Paper 1. Check this before drawing the tariff diagram — students who cannot identify CS/PS on a basic diagram will not follow the welfare analysis.

### Teaching Sequence

Section header (write three definitions on board) → Three Instruments overview (8 min) → Tariff Diagram step-by-step live build (20–25 min across two phases: draw with narration, then common errors debrief) → Worked numerical example (8 min)

### Timing

Hook: 2 min | Instruction: 20 min | Guided Practice: 8 min | Independent: 5 min | **Total: 35 min**

### Misconceptions to Pre-empt

- **Error:** Students draw the domestic supply curve shifting left when a tariff is introduced, rather than adding a new price line above Pw.
  **Correction:** The domestic supply curve does NOT shift. The tariff raises the world price at which imports enter — it is a price line change, not a supply shifter. Domestic producers move along their existing supply curve in response to the higher price. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2022)*
- **Error:** Axes left unlabelled — 'P' instead of 'Price (£)', 'Q' instead of 'Quantity'.
  **Correction:** Unlabelled axes are an automatic deduction on the mark scheme. Drill axis labelling as a non-negotiable checking step. Peer-mark specifically for this before checking content. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2022)*
- **Error:** Students draw only the revenue rectangle C and omit the deadweight loss triangles B and D.
  **Correction:** The rectangle alone is incomplete. Name the triangles explicitly (B left of C, D right of C). Ask students to shade them in a distinctive colour. State explicitly: 'These triangles are welfare destroyed and transferred to nobody — they are the net loss to the economy.' *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2022)*
- **Error:** Students describe the diagram ('the price rises, imports fall') rather than explaining the mechanism.
  **Correction:** Every diagrammatic change needs a causal chain starting with 'because'. Description earns AO2 credit at best. Mechanism explanation earns AO3. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2022)*
- **Error:** Q3 and Q4 reversed on the X-axis — students place new domestic demand left of new domestic supply.
  **Correction:** Supply increases (domestic producers respond to higher price) and demand decreases (consumers respond to higher price) simultaneously. X-axis order left to right must be: Q1, Q3, Q4, Q2. *(Internal teacher observation)*

### Board Work

Draw tariff diagram in 8 steps. Step 1: axes labelled Price (£) and Quantity. Step 2: S_d and D curves. Step 3: P_w horizontal line below domestic equilibrium. Step 4: Q1 (domestic supply at P_w) and Q2 (domestic demand at P_w), label imports = Q2 - Q1. Step 5: P_w+t line above P_w. Step 6: Q3 (new domestic supply) and Q4 (new domestic demand), label new imports = Q4 - Q3. Order on X-axis: Q1, Q3, Q4, Q2. Step 7: Label welfare areas A (producer gain triangle), B (DWL production triangle), C (government revenue rectangle), D (DWL consumption triangle). Step 8: narrate — consumer surplus loss = A+B+C+D; producer gain = A; government revenue = C; net welfare loss = B+D.

### Differentiation

**Stretch/A*:** Optimal tariff vs prohibitive tariff distinction. Terms of trade argument for a large economy.

**Support:** Provide pre-drawn axes with S_d and D curves already marked. Student adds P_w, P_w+t line, Q labels, and welfare areas. Never hand out a completed diagram — building it is the pedagogy.

## Section 3: Quotas and Export Subsidies

**Section ID:** `S03` | **Est. time:** 20 min

**Spec points:** `3.2.6.2-quotas`, `3.2.6.2-export-subsidies`

### Learning Objectives

- [AO1] Distinguish quotas from tariffs in terms of mechanism, revenue, and transparency
- [AO2] Explain the concept of quota rent and why it matters for welfare comparison
- [AO2] Define export subsidy and explain its effects on domestic producers, consumers in importing countries, and taxpayers
- [AO1] Distinguish expenditure-switching from expenditure-reducing policies with examples of each

### Concepts

**Introduced this section:**
- **Quota** (`C009`): A direct quantitative limit on the volume of imports of a specific good permitted into a country over a given period. Raises the domestic price by restricting supply rather than by adding a tax.
- **Quota Rent** (`C010`): The financial gain captured by the holder of an import licence under a quota regime — the difference between the world price and the higher domestic price, multiplied by the quota volume. Unlike tariff revenue, quota rent is not collected by the government and typically accrues to foreign exporters or licensed importers.
- **Export Subsidy** (`C011`): A government payment to domestic producers per unit exported, lowering the effective cost of production and allowing domestic firms to undercut foreign rivals in export markets. Classified as a prohibited subsidy under WTO rules.
- **Expenditure-Switching Policy** (`C012`): A policy that diverts domestic spending away from imports and towards domestically produced goods by changing relative prices, without necessarily reducing total spending. Examples include tariffs, import quotas, and exchange rate depreciation.
- **Expenditure-Reducing Policy** (`C013`): A policy that reduces total aggregate demand in the economy, thereby lowering the overall level of spending including spending on imports. Examples include deflationary fiscal policy (tax rises, spending cuts) and higher interest rates.

**Reinforced from earlier sections:**
- Tariff (`C005`)
- Consumer Surplus (`C006`)
- Producer Surplus (`C007`)
- Deadweight Loss (`C008`)

### Prerequisite Knowledge

Students must understand the tariff diagram (Section 2) — quota rent only makes sense against the backdrop of area C (government revenue rectangle). Do not teach quotas before the tariff diagram is secure.

### Teaching Sequence

Quotas slide (6 min, emphasise quota rent) → quota vs tariff comparison (2 min) → export subsidies slide (6 min, CAP example) → expenditure-switching vs expenditure-reducing two-column board work (8 min)

### Timing

Hook: 2 min | Instruction: 14 min | Guided Practice: 8 min | Independent: 6 min | **Total: 30 min**

### Misconceptions to Pre-empt

- **Error:** Students assume tariffs and quotas have equivalent effects in all respects.
  **Correction:** The price and quantity effects can be equivalent, but the revenue destination is different. Under a tariff, rectangle C is government revenue. Under a quota, the same rectangle is quota rent — a transfer to foreign exporters or licensed importers, not the state. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2023)*
- **Error:** Students classify currency depreciation as expenditure-reducing rather than expenditure-switching.
  **Correction:** Depreciation changes relative prices of imports and exports — it switches the composition of expenditure (makes imports more expensive, exports cheaper). It does not reduce total expenditure. This is the most common error in expenditure-switching questions. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2023)*

### Board Work

Two-column table on board — Expenditure-Switching (tariff, quota, exchange rate depreciation, export subsidy, VER) vs Expenditure-Reducing (higher income tax, cut in government spending, higher interest rates). Leave visible. Students copy and then categorise 5 new examples verbally.

### Differentiation

**Stretch/A*:** Auction of import licences — government captures quota rent. Why is this rarely done in practice?

**Support:** Draw the quota diagram alongside the tariff diagram side-by-side, with identical dimensions. Shade area C in two different colours — one labelled 'government revenue (tariff)', one labelled 'quota rent (quota)'. The structural comparison is visual.

## Section 4: Why Countries Use Protection

**Section ID:** `S04` | **Est. time:** 20 min

**Spec points:** `3.2.6.2-protectionism-causes`, `3.2.6.2-protectionism-consequences`, `3.2.6.2-uk-trade-patterns`, `3.2.6.2-costs-of-trade`

### Learning Objectives

- [AO1] Identify and define the main arguments for and against protectionism
- [AO3] Analyse the infant industry argument with its required conditions
- [AO3] Explain the terms of trade argument for a large economy
- [AO2] Distinguish trade creation from trade diversion as welfare effects of a customs union

### Concepts

**Introduced this section:**
- **Infant Industry Argument** (`C021`): The case for temporary trade protection for a new domestic industry that cannot yet survive foreign competition due to lack of economies of scale or accumulated learning. Protection is justified only if capital market failure prevents private financing, government can identify industries with genuine long-run comparative advantage, and a credible exit mechanism exists.
- **Protectionism** (`C022`): The use of trade policy instruments — tariffs, quotas, export subsidies, voluntary export restraints, and non-tariff barriers — to restrict imports and shield domestic industries from foreign competition.
- **Terms of Trade** (`C014`): The ratio of a country's export prices to its import prices, expressed as an index. An improvement in the terms of trade means a country can obtain more imports per unit of exports. A large-country tariff may improve the terms of trade if foreign exporters reduce prices to maintain market access.

**Reinforced from earlier sections:**
- Comparative Advantage (`C001`)
- Opportunity Cost (`C003`)
- Tariff (`C005`)
- Deadweight Loss (`C008`)
- Expenditure-Switching Policy (`C012`)

### Prerequisite Knowledge

Students should have the tariff diagram secure. The infant industry argument uses the concept of economies of scale (Paper 1). The terms of trade argument requires understanding of relative prices.

### Teaching Sequence

Section header + framing ('teach these as exam paragraphs, not bullet points') → two-column arguments slide (8 min) → infant industry in depth (10 min, including South Korea example and conditions analysis) → terms of trade slide (3 min) → trade creation/diversion slide (4 min)

### Timing

Hook: 2 min | Instruction: 15 min | Guided Practice: 7 min | Independent: 6 min | **Total: 30 min**

### Misconceptions to Pre-empt

- **Error:** Students present the infant industry argument as always valid, without stating the required conditions.
  **Correction:** The argument has three failure conditions: (1) government information problem — can it identify industries with genuine long-run comparative advantage? (2) political economy of exit — once protection is in place, who removes it? (3) capital market failure must actually be present. Each condition is a point of failure. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2022)*
- **Error:** Students treat the terms of trade argument as applicable to the UK without qualification.
  **Correction:** The terms of trade argument only holds for large countries with market power in specific goods. The UK is a small open economy in most goods markets. Apply this argument with explicit caveats in any UK context question. *(Internal teacher observation)*

### Board Work

Write the three conditions for the infant industry argument as a numbered list and leave visible: (1) Government can identify industries with genuine comparative advantage (information problem). (2) Protection is genuinely temporary — credible exit mechanism. (3) Capital market failure prevents private financing.

### Differentiation

**Stretch/A*:** Terms of trade improvement argument — when does it apply? Why is it the 'economist's case for protectionism'? Dynamic vs static analysis of the infant industry case.

**Support:** Scaffold the arguments using a three-column table: Argument | Mechanism | Counterargument. Students complete the mechanism and counterargument columns as you work through each argument.

## Section 5: The Global Trading System

**Section ID:** `S05` | **Est. time:** 20 min

**Spec points:** `3.2.6.2-customs-union`, `3.2.6.2-single-european-market`, `3.2.6.2-wto`

### Learning Objectives

- [AO1] Distinguish free trade area, customs union, single market, and currency union
- [AO2] Define trade creation and trade diversion and explain their welfare implications
- [AO2] Explain the WTO's role, its MFN principle, and the limits of its current enforcement capacity
- [AO3] Apply customs union analysis to the UK's post-Brexit trade position

### Concepts

**Introduced this section:**
- **Trade Creation** (`C015`): The replacement of higher-cost domestic production by lower-cost imports from a partner country following the formation of a customs union. Trade creation increases economic efficiency and is a welfare gain from joining a customs union.
- **Trade Diversion** (`C016`): The replacement of lower-cost imports from a non-member country by higher-cost imports from a partner country following the formation of a customs union, due to the common external tariff making non-member goods more expensive. Trade diversion is a welfare loss from joining a customs union.
- **Customs Union** (`C017`): A trade bloc in which member countries remove internal tariffs and quotas between themselves and adopt a common external tariff (CET) applied to imports from non-member countries. Members cannot negotiate independent trade deals with non-members.
- **Common External Tariff** (`C018`): A uniform tariff rate applied by all members of a customs union to imports from countries outside the union. The CET is the defining feature that distinguishes a customs union from a free trade area.
- **Single European Market** (`C019`): The EU's internal market providing free movement of goods, services, capital, and labour between member states. Goes beyond a customs union by removing non-tariff barriers (regulatory divergence, product standards) and allowing free movement of factors of production.
- **World Trade Organisation** (`C020`): An international organisation with 164 member countries that promotes multilateral trade liberalisation through negotiating rounds, binds tariff levels via the Most Favoured Nation principle, and provides a Dispute Settlement Body to adjudicate trade disputes.

**Reinforced from earlier sections:**
- Tariff (`C005`)
- Quota (`C009`)
- Export Subsidy (`C011`)
- Protectionism (`C022`)

### Prerequisite Knowledge

General Brexit awareness. Students do not need detailed UK political knowledge, but should know that the UK left the EU in 2021 and now has the UK-EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement.

### Teaching Sequence

Four-structure comparison table on board (FTA / Customs Union / Single Market / Currency Union) — 5 min diagnostic, 5 min correction → Customs unions AQA requirements slide (5 min) → US-China case study (6 min) → Post-Brexit UK trade slide (6 min) → WTO role/limits (5 min)

### Timing

Hook: 5 min | Instruction: 15 min | Guided Practice: 5 min | Independent: 5 min | **Total: 30 min**

### Misconceptions to Pre-empt

- **Error:** Students conflate currency union with customs union — answering questions about customs unions by writing about shared currencies.
  **Correction:** A customs union requires: free internal trade + common external tariff. That is all. No shared currency, no free movement of factors. The EU is a customs union AND a single market AND (for eurozone members) a currency union — three separate things. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2023)*
- **Error:** Students describe the WTO as simply 'banning tariffs'.
  **Correction:** The WTO does NOT ban tariffs. It binds their level (members agree maximum bound rates), prohibits discrimination (MFN principle), and provides dispute settlement. Tariffs under bound rates are legal. Anti-dumping tariffs, national security tariffs, and infant industry protections for developing nations are all permitted. *(AQA Examiner Report Jun 2022)*

### Board Work

Four-row comparison table: FTA (internal tariffs removed, no CET, no factor movement, no currency) vs Customs Union (add CET) vs Single Market (add factor movement) vs Currency Union (add shared currency). Place EU at appropriate rows as you build it. Brexit moved UK from Single Market to FTA position (with the TCA).

### Differentiation

**Stretch/A*:** Trade creation vs trade diversion — in which circumstances does a customs union produce a net welfare gain? (When trade creation > trade diversion, i.e. the CET is not so high that it diverts large volumes from efficient non-members.)

**Support:** Focus on the four-structure table — students who cannot populate this from memory cannot answer customs union questions. Test it twice in the lesson.

## Section 6: Liberation Day 2025 — A Live Case Study

**Section ID:** `S05b` | **Est. time:** 30 min

**Spec points:** `3.2.6.2-tariffs`, `3.2.6.2-protectionism-causes`, `3.2.6.2-protectionism-consequences`, `3.2.6.2-wto`

### Learning Objectives

- [AO2] Apply the tariff welfare diagram to a real-world example using actual US tariff rate data
- [AO3] Analyse the macroeconomic channels through which trade policy changes affect equity markets and household welfare
- [AO4] Evaluate competing economic arguments for and against the US tariff decision using structured AO4 chains of reasoning
- [AO3] Identify MFN implications of the UK-US bilateral deal and assess whether it represents genuine free trade

### Concepts

**Introduced this section:**
- **Most Favoured Nation (MFN) Principle** (`C023`): The WTO rule requiring that any trade advantage given to one WTO member must be extended immediately and unconditionally to all WTO members. Prohibits selective tariff rates between individual trading partners except where a free trade agreement or customs union has been formally notified under WTO Article XXIV.
- **Retaliatory Tariff** (`C024`): A tariff imposed by one country in direct response to a tariff or trade restriction imposed by a trading partner. Retaliation escalates trade disputes into trade wars — where both countries impose tariffs, both populations pay higher prices, and total trade volume contracts. Historical examples: US-China 2018-2025; EU-US steel dispute 2002; Smoot-Hawley 1930.

**Reinforced from earlier sections:**
- Tariff (`C005`)
- Consumer Surplus (`C006`)
- Producer Surplus (`C007`)
- Deadweight Loss (`C008`)
- Terms of Trade (`C014`)
- World Trade Organisation (`C020`)
- Infant Industry Argument (`C021`)
- Protectionism (`C022`)

### Prerequisite Knowledge

Students must have completed S02 (tariff diagram), S04 (arguments for/against protectionism), and S05 (WTO + global trading system). This section synthesises all prior learning into a single real-world application. It does not introduce new theory.

### Teaching Sequence

1. Hook: display a market chart and ask what caused it (2 min). 2. Tell the Liberation Day story chronologically (3 min). 3. Students draw the tariff diagram for US auto imports from memory (8 min). 4. Discussion: why did markets fall? Elicit both channels (5 min). 5. UK angle: good deal or window dressing? Pair debate (5 min). 6. AO4 framework: build the evaluation together as a class (7 min).

### Timing

Hook: 2 min | Narrative: 3 min | Diagram Application: 8 min | Discussion: 5 min | Uk Angle: 5 min | Evaluation Framework: 7 min | **Total: 30 min**

### Misconceptions to Pre-empt

- **Error:** Confusing the effective tariff rate with individual sector rates: '27% was the tariff on all goods.' In fact, 27% was the weighted average — sector rates varied (cars: 25%, steel: 50%, electronics: 0-10%).
  **Correction:** The US effective tariff rate is a weighted average. Use sector-specific rates when given in a question. The exam may provide stimulus data with specific rates — use those, not the aggregate. *(Anticipated exam trap in data-response questions)*
- **Error:** Writing 'markets fell because investors were worried' — circular and earns zero AO3 marks.
  **Correction:** Markets fell because: (1) higher import costs reduce profit margins for US firms using imported components; (2) retaliatory tariffs reduce export revenue; (3) supply chain uncertainty raises the discount rate on future earnings. Name the mechanism. *(AQA AO3 chains of reasoning requirement)*

### Board Work

Timeline on the left: 2 Apr 2025 announcement -> 3 Apr S&P -4.84% -> 9 Apr 90-day pause -> 20 Feb 2026 court ruling. On the right: blank tariff diagram axes. Leave both visible throughout.

### Differentiation

**Stretch/A*:** Research the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act (1930) and the 65% fall in world trade that followed. Is 2025 comparable? What structural differences (WTO, bilateral deals, digital trade) might limit contagion? Write a comparative paragraph.

**Support:** Provide a partially completed tariff diagram with Pw already marked and axes labelled. Students add Pw+T, Q1-Q4, and the six welfare areas.

The Liberation Day data will date — update the US effective tariff rate and market figures each academic year. The diagram mechanics and evaluation framework are timeless and transferable to any tariff scenario.

### Misconceptions to Pre-empt

- **Error:** Describing the tariff diagram ('the price rises, imports fall') rather than explaining the economic mechanism.
  **Correction:** Every diagrammatic change needs a causal chain starting with 'because'. Description earns AO2 at best. 'Consumer surplus falls BECAUSE the higher tariff-inclusive price reduces quantity demanded along the demand curve, and some consumers who were willing to pay above the world price but below the domestic tariff price no longer purchase the good.' *(AQA Economics Paper 2 Examiner Report Jun 2022)*
- **Error:** Confusing the current account deficit (trade imbalance) with the government budget deficit (fiscal imbalance).
  **Correction:** Write both definitions side by side and leave visible: CURRENT ACCOUNT DEFICIT = national spending on imports > earnings from exports. BUDGET DEFICIT = government spending > tax revenue. Different entities, different transactions, different policy levers. They can move in opposite directions. *(AQA Economics Paper 2 Examiner Report Jun 2023)*
- **Error:** Answering customs union questions by writing about currency unions.
  **Correction:** Customs union = free internal trade + common external tariff. Currency union = customs union + shared currency. These are structurally different. The four-structure table (FTA / CU / SEM / Currency Union) must be known from memory. *(AQA Economics Paper 2 Examiner Report Jun 2023)*

### Board Work

Write both deficit definitions side-by-side and leave visible for the entire session. This is the single most recurring multi-subject confusion across all ability bands.
