## 1. What is Protectionism?

In March 2018, President Trump announced a 25% tariff on all imported steel and a 10% tariff on imported aluminium. The stated justification was national security: American steel plants were closing, steelworkers were losing jobs, and the US was becoming dependent on Chinese and foreign supply chains for a material essential to defence manufacturing. Within weeks, Canada, the EU, and China had all announced retaliatory tariffs on American goods — bourbon, motorcycles, orange juice. A policy sold as protecting American jobs triggered a trade war that raised costs for American manufacturers who needed cheap steel as an input. By 2019, the IMF estimated the conflict had reduced global output by around 0.5% of GDP.

That sequence — protection, retaliation, rising costs for domestic producers who depend on imports, reduced global output — is the story of protectionism in a paragraph. Understanding it properly is the goal of this unit.

**Protectionism** is government policy that restricts international trade to give domestic producers a competitive advantage over foreign rivals. The instruments are tariffs, quotas, and export subsidies (and, less obviously, exchange rate manipulation, regulatory barriers, and supply-side subsidies — though the first three are the main examination focus).

The underlying tension is always the same: free trade benefits consumers and the global economy in aggregate, but the costs and benefits are unevenly distributed. Domestic producers who face foreign competition lose. Domestic consumers who buy imported goods gain. Governments face political pressure from producers and workers who lose, not from the diffuse majority who gain slightly as individual consumers. This asymmetry between concentrated producer losses and dispersed consumer gains explains why protectionist pressure is a permanent feature of democratic politics, even when the economics of free trade are well-established.

## 2. The Three Main Instruments

### Tariffs

A **tariff** is a tax levied on imported goods at the point of entry into a country. It can be expressed as a percentage of the import price (an *ad valorem* tariff — e.g. 25% on steel) or as a fixed amount per unit (a *specific* tariff — e.g. £50 per tonne of coal). The effect is to raise the domestic price of the imported good above the world price.

#### The Tariff Diagram — Built Step by Step

The tariff diagram is the single most important piece of analysis in this unit. Examiners report that students lose marks year after year by drawing it carelessly or describing it rather than explaining it. Read what follows, then draw the diagram from scratch.

**Step 1 — Set up your axes.**
Draw a standard supply and demand diagram. Label the vertical axis **Price (£)** and the horizontal axis **Quantity**. Draw a downward-sloping domestic demand curve D and an upward-sloping domestic supply curve S. These represent domestic demand and domestic production capacity.

**Step 2 — Draw the world price.**
Draw a horizontal line cutting across the diagram at a price below the intersection of D and S. Label this line **Pw** (world price). It is horizontal because the country is a small open economy — its purchasing decisions do not affect the world price (it is a price-taker in the international market).

**Step 3 — Show the free trade outcome.**
At world price Pw, domestic consumers demand quantity **Q2** (read off the demand curve). Domestic producers only supply quantity **Q1** (read off the supply curve — they cannot profitably produce beyond Q1 at the low world price). The gap between Q1 and Q2 is **imports under free trade**.

**Step 4 — Introduce the tariff.**
Draw a second horizontal line above Pw. Label it **Pw + tariff**. This is the new domestic price after the tax is applied to imports.

**Step 5 — Show the post-tariff outcome.**
At the higher price Pw + tariff, domestic consumers now demand only **Q3** (demand has contracted along the demand curve). Domestic producers now supply **Q4** (supply has expanded — higher price makes more domestic production profitable). Imports have fallen: the new import volume is the gap between Q4 and Q3.

**Step 6 — Label the welfare areas.** This is where most marks are. Using the space between Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4, and the two price lines, identify four distinct areas:

- **Producer surplus gain (area A):** The rectangle between Pw and Pw+tariff, from Q1 downward to the supply curve, up to Q4. Domestic producers receive a higher price for every unit they were already producing and can now profitably produce more units. This is a transfer from consumers to producers.

- **Government revenue (area B):** The rectangle between Pw and Pw+tariff, over the quantity of imports that still occur (Q4 to Q3). Every imported unit is taxed, so the government collects revenue equal to the tariff × post-tariff import volume. This is a transfer from consumers to government.

- **Deadweight loss — production inefficiency (area C, a triangle):** The triangle immediately to the right of Q1, between Pw and Pw+tariff, up to the supply curve. This represents resources used to produce domestically at a cost above the world price. Society uses more resources than necessary to produce these units — the domestic producer is less efficient than the foreign supplier, but is protected from that competition. This output would have been imported more cheaply; instead it is produced domestically at higher cost.

- **Deadweight loss — consumption inefficiency (area D, a triangle):** The triangle to the right of Q3, between the demand curve and Pw+tariff. This represents consumption that does not occur because the tariff has raised the price above what some consumers were willing to pay. These consumers valued the good above the world price but below the domestic price with tariff — they would have bought under free trade, but do not buy now. Their lost consumer surplus is gone entirely — it is not transferred anywhere.

**Total consumer surplus loss = A + B + C + D.** Of this, A goes to domestic producers, B goes to the government, and C + D are deadweight losses — welfare destroyed and not transferred to anyone.

> **Exam technique — 9-mark diagram questions.** In a 9-mark question, there are no marks for evaluation — only for explaining the process clearly with a correctly drawn and labelled diagram. Do not spend time qualifying your answer ("however, this depends on..."). Spend it ensuring you explain what happens to each area: what it represents economically, who gains or loses, and why. The mark scheme rewards explanation of mechanism, not judgement.

> **Exam technique — what examiners penalise in diagram questions.** Axes not labelled: immediate mark loss. Treating the tariff as a shift of the supply curve rather than a new price line: wrong model. Confusing tariff *introduction* with tariff *reduction*: different direction of change to every area. When you practise past papers, draw the diagram first, then write — not simultaneously.

#### Effects of a Tariff on Each Group

**Domestic consumers** are unambiguously worse off. They pay a higher price for every unit they purchase, and some consumers stop buying the good altogether. Total consumer surplus falls by A + B + C + D.

**Domestic producers** gain. The tariff raises the domestic price above the world price, allowing less efficient domestic producers to remain competitive. Producer surplus increases by area A. Employment in the protected industry is sustained or increased.

**Government** collects revenue equal to area B. This can be used to fund public services, reduce other taxes, or subsidise other industries — it is not wasted, but it represents a transfer from consumers.

**Foreign exporters** are worse off. Their exports to the country fall (imports have decreased). If the country is large enough to influence world prices, the world price itself may fall slightly, partly offsetting the tariff — but for a small open economy, world price is unchanged and foreign exporters simply sell less.

**Overall welfare in the importing country** falls by the two deadweight loss triangles (C + D). Even accounting for the redistribution to producers (A) and government (B), the country as a whole is poorer because resources are being used inefficiently.

#### Worked Numerical Example

Suppose the world price of a good is £10. The domestic government imposes a tariff of £4 per unit. The new domestic price is £14.

At £10, domestic producers supply 100 units and domestic consumers demand 300 units. Imports = 200 units.

At £14, domestic producers supply 140 units and domestic consumers demand 260 units. Imports = 120 units.

Government revenue = tariff × post-tariff import volume = £4 × 120 = **£480**.

Producer surplus gain = £4 × (100 + 140) ÷ 2 = £4 × 120 = **£480** (approximately, assuming linear supply).

Consumer surplus loss = £4 × (300 + 260) ÷ 2 = £4 × 280 = **£1,120** (approximately).

Deadweight loss ≈ £1,120 − £480 (producer gain) − £480 (government revenue) = **£160**.

> **Exam technique — calculation questions.** Always show your working. Always include units (£, "units", "billion"). A correct answer with no working scores fewer marks than a correct method with a minor arithmetic error.

### Quotas

A **quota** is a direct limit on the quantity of a good that can be imported over a given time period. Where a tariff raises the price and lets the quantity adjust, a quota fixes the quantity and lets the price adjust.

The key mechanical difference from a tariff: the domestic price still rises above the world price (as with a tariff), but the government collects no revenue from this. Under a tariff, area B goes to the government. Under a quota, area B becomes a gain to whoever holds the import licences — typically the foreign exporters themselves, or importers who were lucky enough to be allocated licences. This is sometimes called **quota rent**.

In a tariff diagram adapted for a quota, you draw the same four areas (A, B, C, D), but area B is labelled as quota rent (accruing to licence holders) rather than government revenue. Otherwise the consumer and welfare effects are identical in direction, though not necessarily in magnitude — a government can fine-tune the price effect by choosing the tariff level, but with a quota it is harder to predict exactly what the domestic price will be, especially if demand shifts.

Quotas are generally considered less transparent than tariffs. The WTO has pushed for "tariffication" — converting quotas into equivalent tariffs — precisely because tariffs are more visible, more measurable, and more easily negotiated downward in trade rounds.

### Export Subsidies

An **export subsidy** is a payment by a government to domestic producers for every unit they export. The effect is to allow domestic exporters to sell abroad at below their cost of production, making their goods artificially cheap in foreign markets.

The exporting country's government bears the cost of the subsidy (taxpayers pay). Domestic producers benefit — they receive a higher effective price (market price + subsidy). Foreign consumers buying the subsidised good benefit in the short run from lower prices. But domestic producers in the *importing* country are harmed — they cannot compete with artificially cheap imports.

The EU's Common Agricultural Policy historically provided export subsidies for European agricultural products, allowing European farmers to sell grain, butter, and sugar on world markets at below world prices. This depressed global commodity prices and made it impossible for developing-country farmers to compete — a significant issue for agricultural economies in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.

The WTO Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures classifies export subsidies as "prohibited subsidies" — the most serious category of trade-distorting measures. Countries can challenge export subsidies at the WTO Dispute Settlement Body and, if the case is upheld, apply countervailing duties to offset the subsidy's effect. The EU formally ended most agricultural export subsidies in 2013 under WTO pressure, though domestic production subsidies (which have a similar distorting effect) remain.

> **Exam technique.** The examiner wants you to distinguish clearly between a tariff (which raises revenue for the importing country's government), a quota (which generates quota rent but no government revenue), and an export subsidy (which costs the exporting country's government money). Students who conflate these three instruments are answering at the lowest level-of-response band.

## 3. Why Do Countries Use Protectionism? (The Arguments for Protection)

### The Infant Industry Argument

This is the oldest and most academically respectable argument for protection. It runs as follows: a new domestic industry cannot initially compete with established foreign rivals because it has not yet achieved economies of scale, its workforce lacks the relevant skills, and it has not yet moved down the learning curve in production techniques. If the industry is exposed to full foreign competition in its early years, it will be bankrupted before it can develop — even if, once mature, it could eventually be internationally competitive. Temporary protection — a tariff or quota for a defined period — gives the infant industry time to grow, achieve scale, and reduce costs to internationally competitive levels. Once it has matured, protection can be withdrawn.

The argument has genuine theoretical merit. South Korean industrial policy in the 1960s–80s is the standard example: targeted protection and state support for industries such as steel (POSCO), semiconductors (Samsung), and shipbuilding allowed South Korea to develop internationally competitive industries that now require no protection. Japan's post-war industrial policy followed a similar model.

**When does the infant industry argument fail?** The argument requires several conditions to hold simultaneously, and in practice they often do not.

First, government must correctly identify which industries will eventually become competitive — a problem of information that markets arguably handle better than bureaucrats. Governments back losers as often as winners. Brazil's effort to protect its domestic computer industry in the 1980s kept domestic computing costs far above world levels for a decade while preventing its economy from accessing internationally competitive technology.

Second, the protection must be temporary. Industries that receive protection rarely lobby to have it removed once they are no longer "infants." Political economy works against exit: once a protected industry employs workers and generates political donations, withdrawing protection is extremely difficult. The "infant" becomes a permanent dependent.

Third, if the future economies of scale are genuinely achievable, a firm could in principle borrow to finance its early loss-making years and repay the debt from future profits — as a private investor would. The need for government protection implies either capital market failure (banks won't lend to the industry) or that the expected future profits are insufficient to justify the investment, in which case protection is misallocating resources.

Fourth, even where protection succeeds in building a domestic industry, the cost to consumers during the protection period must be weighed against the future gains. If it takes 20 years of protection at significant consumer cost to develop an industry that then operates at world-competitive prices, the net present value of that strategy may still be negative.

> **Exam technique — evaluation in 25-mark questions.** The strongest responses do not just list counterarguments. They specify the *conditions* under which the infant industry argument works (identifiable dynamic comparative advantage, credible exit rule, capital market failure) and the conditions under which it fails (government information problems, political economy of exit, viable private financing alternative). "It depends on whether..." is the structure of high-band evaluation.

### Protection of Jobs and Strategic Industries

The employment argument is the most politically powerful justification for protection, even if it is the least economically robust. When a domestic industry faces import competition and domestic workers face redundancy, protection keeps jobs in place. The political salience of visible, concentrated job losses — a steel mill closing, a car plant relocating — far exceeds the diffuse, invisible gains to consumers from cheaper imports.

Economists typically counter that free trade creates as many jobs as it destroys — resources released from contracting industries move into expanding ones where the country has comparative advantage. But this relies on labour mobility that may not exist in practice: a 50-year-old steelworker in Sheffield in 1984 could not easily retrain as a software engineer. In communities where a single industry dominates, import-driven decline creates long-term unemployment, falling public health, and social fragmentation that persist for decades. The economist's long run can be brutal for specific people in specific places.

The **strategic industries** variant of this argument adds national security. Some industries are considered too important to be allowed to disappear, regardless of comparative advantage: defence manufacturing, food production, energy, and (increasingly) semiconductors and advanced computing. A country that allows its domestic steel, pharmaceutical, or microchip industry to be eliminated by foreign competition may face a severe vulnerability in a crisis — as became apparent globally during the COVID-19 pandemic when countries found themselves dependent on foreign supply chains for PPE, vaccines, and medical equipment.

### Retaliation

If a trading partner imposes unfair trade restrictions, a country may retaliate with its own tariffs — not because protection is economically efficient, but as a strategic bargaining tool. The logic is that the threat of retaliation deters the partner from imposing barriers in the first place.

This has the structure of a **prisoner's dilemma** in game theory. Both countries benefit from mutual free trade (the cooperative equilibrium). But if one country can gain by imposing barriers while the other maintains free trade, there is an incentive to defect. If both defect (both impose barriers), both are worse off than mutual free trade — but the Nash equilibrium (each playing their dominant strategy independently) results in mutual protection anyway.

This is why the WTO exists: as an institutional mechanism for maintaining the cooperative equilibrium. By providing a dispute resolution process and a credible threat of authorised retaliation, the WTO makes it easier for countries to commit to free trade and harder for any single country to defect without consequences.

The US-China trade war from 2018 illustrates both the logic and the problem. US tariffs triggered Chinese retaliation, which triggered further US escalation. Both economies suffered. American soybean farmers — who had no competitive grievance with China — found their primary export market closed by retaliatory tariffs because they happened to be politically valuable targets in US swing states.

### Correcting a Balance of Payments Deficit

A persistent current account deficit means that a country is spending more on imports than it earns from exports. This is unsustainable in the long run because it must be financed by borrowing or asset sales. One policy response is **expenditure-switching** — redirecting domestic spending away from imports and towards domestically produced goods. Tariffs and quotas do this by raising the price of imports relative to domestic substitutes.

However, this argument is more defensible as a short-term emergency measure than as a long-term strategy. Tariffs address the symptom (excess imports) rather than the cause, which is typically that domestic producers are uncompetitive in terms of price, quality, or productivity. The appropriate long-run response is supply-side policy to improve competitiveness, not perpetual protection.

There is also a risk of a **fallacy of composition**: if every deficit country imposes import restrictions simultaneously, total global trade falls without any country improving its relative position. This is the protectionist spiral that deepened the Great Depression in the 1930s, when the US Smoot-Hawley tariff triggered a cascade of retaliatory restrictions that collapsed world trade by around 65% between 1929 and 1934.

> **Exam technique — a persistent examiner error.** Do not confuse the current account deficit with the government budget deficit. They are completely different things with different causes and different policy responses. The current account deficit is an international trade imbalance; the budget deficit is a domestic fiscal imbalance. This confusion is flagged in every recent examiner report as a common error costing students marks.

### Anti-Dumping

**Dumping** occurs when a foreign producer sells goods in an export market at a price below the cost of production, or below the price charged in its home market. The motivation is usually to eliminate domestic competition in the importing market and then raise prices once domestic rivals have been driven out — a form of predatory pricing.

The WTO permits anti-dumping duties (a form of tariff) where a country can demonstrate that dumping is occurring and causing material injury to domestic producers. In practice, anti-dumping duties are frequently misused as a disguised form of protection: the threshold for "material injury" is set loosely, and politically well-connected domestic industries can obtain anti-dumping protection even when no genuine predatory pricing is occurring.

China has been the most frequent target of anti-dumping investigations — by the EU, the US, and other economies — particularly in steel, solar panels, and electric vehicles. The EU imposed anti-dumping tariffs of up to 47% on Chinese electric vehicles in 2024, arguing that Chinese state subsidies constituted dumping. China denied this and initiated its own WTO dispute.

### Revenue Raising

For less-developed economies with limited capacity to administer income taxes or VAT, import tariffs on goods entering at a small number of ports are administratively easier to collect and can provide a significant share of government revenue. This was historically the primary revenue source for many developing nations — the US itself raised most of its federal revenue from tariffs throughout the 19th century. It is the least intellectually impressive justification for protection but is practically significant for the subset of economies where it applies.

## 4. Consequences of Protectionism

### For the Importing Country

Consumers pay higher prices for protected goods. Consumer surplus falls. If the protected goods are intermediate inputs for other industries — as steel is for car manufacturing, construction, and engineering — then protection in one sector raises costs throughout the downstream supply chain, harming domestic industries that use the protected input. American manufacturers who used steel as an input were damaged by the 2018 steel tariffs; the number of jobs in steel-using industries exceeded the number in steel-producing industries by roughly 80:1.

Domestic producers in the protected industry benefit in the short run: producer surplus rises, employment is sustained. But protection reduces the pressure to innovate and improve productivity. Over time, a protected industry may fall further behind international competition, not catch up — particularly if the infant industry argument does not apply and the industry has no plausible path to competitive efficiency.

The government gains tariff revenue (not from quotas). This is a transfer, not a net welfare gain — it comes entirely from consumers.

### For the Exporting Country

Foreign producers lose export markets. If the exporting country is a developing nation that is competitive in labour-intensive manufacturing or agriculture, protectionism by rich nations directly harms its economic development. Oxfam has estimated that EU and US agricultural subsidies and protection cost developing-country agricultural exporters more than $50 billion per year in lost export earnings — considerably more than the total aid these countries receive.

The exporting country may also retaliate, imposing its own restrictions and triggering the escalation cycle described above.

### For Global Welfare

Global welfare falls because trade moves away from the pattern of comparative advantage — goods are produced by less efficient domestic producers rather than more efficient foreign ones. Deadweight losses accumulate across multiple countries and multiple product markets. The WTO estimates that eliminating all remaining global trade barriers would add trillions of dollars to global GDP.

Retaliation cycles are the most dangerous consequence. When major trading partners escalate in sequence — as happened in 1930s and again in the US-China trade war — the cumulative damage far exceeds the initial bilateral dispute.

### For Less-Developed Countries Specifically

Rich-country protectionism is most damaging to developing nations for two reasons. First, developing countries often have comparative advantage in the very sectors — agriculture, basic manufacturing, textiles — where rich countries protect most heavily. The EU's Common External Tariff imposes escalating tariffs on processed agricultural products, making it easier to export raw commodities (low value-added) than processed food (high value-added) — structurally locking developing exporters into the lower end of global value chains.

Second, developing countries have less capacity to retaliate or litigate at the WTO — dispute settlement is expensive and technically demanding, and small economies have less leverage in bilateral negotiations.

## 5. The Case For and Against Free Trade

This is the heart of 25-mark essay territory. The question is never simply "list advantages of free trade" or "list disadvantages of protection" — it is always an *evaluation* of arguments, recognising the conditions under which each holds and the circumstances under which they break down.

### The Core Case for Free Trade

The foundational argument is **comparative advantage** (David Ricardo, 1817). Even if one country is absolutely more efficient than another at producing every good, both countries gain from specialising in the goods where their *relative* efficiency is highest and trading for the rest. This is counterintuitive but mathematically robust: total global output is maximised when each country specialises in its comparative advantage. Free trade, by enabling this specialisation, increases the total size of the global economic pie.

To this add **economies of scale**: by accessing a global market rather than just a domestic one, firms can operate at much larger scales, reducing average costs. British car manufacturers in the 1970s produced for a domestic market; German and Japanese manufacturers produced for the world. The difference in scale partly explains the difference in long-run competitiveness. Trade liberalisation allows firms to expand output and descend the long-run average cost curve.

**Increased competition** disciplines domestic firms that might otherwise operate as oligopolies or monopolies behind protective barriers. Import competition forces firms to innovate, cut costs, and improve quality. The UK's domestic car industry in the 1970s was substantially protected by both formal and informal barriers; its eventual collapse owed much to the accumulated failure to innovate under the lack of competitive pressure.

**Access to a wider variety of goods** at lower prices benefits consumers directly. Trade allows consumers in cold climates to eat tropical fruits year-round; allows economies without domestic semiconductor industries to access the latest electronics; allows countries with expensive domestic energy to import cheaper alternatives.

### Legitimate Arguments Against Completely Free Trade

The **infant industry argument** has genuine merit in limited, well-specified circumstances — as discussed in Section 3. Where there is a clear path to competitive efficiency, a capital market failure preventing private financing, and a credible government commitment to temporary (not permanent) protection, infant industry protection may be welfare-improving in the long run, even if costly in the short run.

**Strategic industries and national security** represent a legitimate qualification to comparative advantage. The principle of comparative advantage assumes that the world price is always accessible — that a country can always import what it does not produce. This assumption fails in genuine emergencies (war, pandemic, supply chain disruption). A country that has no domestic food, energy, or defence production capacity is strategically vulnerable in a way that a comparative advantage calculation does not capture.

**The distribution of gains from trade** is a serious objection to purely free-trade advocacy. Even if total gains from trade are positive, they are unevenly distributed: workers and communities in import-competing industries bear the costs, while gains are diffuse and spread thinly across millions of consumers. If the losers are geographically concentrated (a former industrial region), lack skills to transition to growing sectors, and are not adequately compensated by redistribution of the gains, then free trade can produce both economic efficiency and social dislocation simultaneously.

**Terms of trade effects** provide a theoretical argument for an *optimal tariff* in large economies. If a country is large enough to influence world prices (a non-price-taker), imposing a tariff may improve its terms of trade by depressing the price at which it imports. The welfare gain from improved terms of trade may exceed the domestic deadweight loss — making the tariff welfare-improving for the large country, at the expense of the rest of the world. This is a valid theoretical argument but invites retaliation, and in practice most countries are not large enough for the effect to be significant.

### The Strongest Evaluation

The most persuasive evaluation does not choose a winner but specifies conditions. Free trade is the right presumption for most goods in most circumstances, because the evidence for comparative advantage gains is strong and the evidence for successful selective protection is weak. But the infant industry argument is genuinely defensible where capital markets are failing and government has strong information about a viable industry. Strategic industry protection is genuinely defensible where supply chain vulnerability poses real security risks. And the distributional case for compensating trade's losers — through active labour market policy, investment in affected communities, and income support — is overwhelming, even for economists who otherwise strongly support free trade.

The weakest essays take one side and dismiss the other. The strongest essays identify the margins where arguments shift: the size of the economy, the availability of capital markets, the tradability of future gains, the state's information advantage, the credibility of exit commitments, and the adequacy of redistribution mechanisms.

> **Exam technique — 25-mark essay structure.** The top mark band requires "evaluation throughout and in a conclusion" — not a final paragraph of evaluation tacked onto a list of arguments. This means each argument should be qualified as you make it: "The infant industry argument is compelling *when* capital markets are failing and *when* government can identify a viable industry — but less so when politicians protect incumbents to preserve votes." Work the conditions into each analytical paragraph, then draw them together in a conclusion that makes a specific, justified judgement.

## 6. Current Real-World Context

Essays at the top mark band require contextual grounding in real-world examples. These four cases are the most useful for Paper 2 protectionism questions. Learn the key facts, not just the names.

### The US-China Trade War (2018–present)

In January 2018, the Trump administration began imposing tariffs on Chinese goods, citing unfair trade practices: state subsidies to Chinese exporters, forced technology transfer requirements on US firms operating in China, and intellectual property theft. Initial tariffs of 25% on $34 billion of Chinese goods escalated through 2018–19 to cover over $350 billion of imports.

China retaliated with equivalent tariffs on US agricultural exports, hitting soybean farmers, pork producers, and lobster exporters. The "Phase One" deal of January 2020 paused escalation in exchange for Chinese commitments to purchase additional US goods, though most tariffs remained in place. The Biden administration largely maintained the tariffs, and in 2024 raised tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles to 100%, solar cells to 50%, and semiconductors to 50%.

Key points for analysis: the tariffs did not substantially reduce the US trade deficit with China (it actually widened in some years, as the deficit with other Asian producers grew); American consumers paid higher prices for a wide range of goods; American firms dependent on Chinese inputs saw costs rise; the conflict accelerated Chinese domestic investment in the targeted sectors (particularly semiconductors), arguably strengthening rather than weakening Chinese technological capacity.

### Post-Brexit UK Trade Policy

Before 2021, the UK's trade policy was set by the EU. As an EU member, the UK applied the **Common External Tariff** (CET) to goods from outside the EU (averaging around 5–6% overall, but much higher in agriculture and textiles) and enjoyed zero tariffs on trade within the Single Market.

Post-Brexit, the UK has set its own Global Tariff schedule, which broadly replicates the CET but with some reductions — the UK has unilaterally reduced tariffs on many goods to zero, including on a range of food and consumer products, making them cheaper to import from anywhere in the world. The UK has also negotiated free trade agreements (FTAs) with Australia, New Zealand, Japan, and the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP).

However, the introduction of new non-tariff barriers between the UK and the EU — customs declarations, rules of origin checks, regulatory divergence — has been estimated to have reduced UK-EU trade in goods by 15–25% compared to the counterfactual of continued EU membership. The Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA) sets tariffs at zero for goods meeting rules of origin requirements, but the administrative burden and border friction functions as a significant non-tariff barrier. Services trade — in which the UK had a strong comparative advantage — was largely excluded from the TCA, with UK-based financial services firms losing passporting rights.

This is a rich case study for the non-tariff barrier concept, the distinction between tariff and non-tariff protectionism, and the trade-off between regulatory autonomy and market access.

### WTO Dispute Cases

The **WTO Dispute Settlement Body** (DSB) is the quasi-judicial mechanism for resolving trade disputes between member states. Any member can bring a complaint if it believes another member has violated WTO agreements. A panel of experts hears the case; if the complaint is upheld, the losing party must bring its measures into compliance or face authorised retaliation.

Relevant recent cases: the **EU-US steel tariff dispute**, in which the EU challenged US steel and aluminium tariffs under WTO agreements, and the WTO ruled in 2022 that the US tariffs violated the Agreement on Safeguards; and the **Airbus/Boeing subsidies dispute**, a 17-year case in which both the EU and US were found to have illegally subsidised their respective aircraft manufacturers — the longest and most complex case in WTO history, finally settled in 2021 with a suspension of retaliatory tariffs.

The DSB's **Appellate Body** — the WTO's appeal court — has been paralysed since 2019 because the US has blocked the appointment of new judges, preventing new appeals being heard. This represents a significant weakening of the international rules-based trading system and reflects US scepticism of multilateral trade governance under both the Trump and Biden administrations.

### The EU Common External Tariff and Effects on Development

The EU applies its Common External Tariff to imports from all non-preferential trading partners. The structure is important: raw materials typically face low or zero tariffs, while processed goods and manufactured products face higher tariffs. This **tariff escalation** structure means that a developing country can export raw cocoa to the EU duty-free but faces a tariff of around 30% on processed chocolate. This structurally discourages developing countries from moving up the value chain and processing their own raw materials domestically.

The EU partially addresses this through its **Generalised System of Preferences (GSP)**, which gives lower tariffs to developing countries, and the **Everything But Arms (EBA)** initiative, which gives least-developed countries (LDCs) zero tariff access for all goods except arms. However, the rules of origin requirements attached to these schemes are complex and often difficult for LDC exporters to meet in practice.

## 7. Key Terms Glossary

**Absolute advantage** — the ability to produce a good using fewer resources than a trading partner, in absolute terms. Distinct from comparative advantage.

**Ad valorem tariff** — a tariff expressed as a percentage of the import price (e.g. 25% on steel imports).

**Anti-dumping duty** — a tariff imposed specifically to offset the price advantage obtained by a foreign producer selling below the cost of production or below its home market price.

**Balance of trade** — the value of a country's exports of goods minus the value of its imports of goods. A component of the current account.

**Comparative advantage** — the ability to produce a good at a lower *opportunity cost* than a trading partner. The basis for mutually beneficial trade even where one country has absolute advantage in everything.

**Common External Tariff (CET)** — the uniform tariff applied by all members of a customs union to imports from non-member countries. The EU's CET means that all EU member states charge the same tariff on imports from, say, the United States.

**Countervailing duty** — a tariff imposed to offset a foreign government subsidy that gives foreign producers an unfair price advantage.

**Current account deficit** — a situation where a country's spending on imports of goods, services, and transfer payments exceeds its earnings from exports and income receipts. Not the same as a government budget deficit.

**Deadweight loss** — the reduction in total economic welfare (consumer plus producer surplus) that is not transferred to any other party. In a tariff diagram, it is represented by the two welfare triangles (areas C and D). It is lost permanently.

**Dumping** — the practice of selling exports at a price below cost of production or below the domestic market price, with the intention of driving out foreign competition.

**Expenditure-switching policy** — a policy that changes the *composition* of domestic spending by making imports more expensive relative to domestic goods, thereby redirecting demand toward domestic production. Tariffs, quotas, and exchange rate depreciation are all expenditure-switching policies.

**Expenditure-reducing policy** — a policy that reduces the *total level* of domestic spending (and therefore imports), such as raising interest rates or cutting government spending. Distinct from expenditure-switching.

**Export subsidy** — a payment by government to domestic producers per unit exported, allowing them to sell in foreign markets at below their cost of production.

**Free trade area (FTA)** — a trading bloc in which member countries remove tariffs on trade between themselves but each member sets its own tariff against non-members.

**Infant industry** — a newly established domestic industry that is not yet competitive with established foreign rivals due to lack of scale, experience, or skills.

**Non-tariff barrier (NTB)** — any trade restriction other than a tariff: e.g. import quotas, regulatory standards, customs procedures, voluntary export restraints, rules of origin requirements.

**Quota** — a direct quantitative limit on the amount of a good that can be imported or exported over a specified period.

**Quota rent** — the economic gain accruing to holders of import licences under a quota. Under a tariff, the equivalent gain goes to the government as revenue; under a quota, it typically accrues to foreign exporters or licence holders.

**Specific tariff** — a tariff expressed as a fixed monetary amount per unit (e.g. £50 per tonne).

**Tariff** — a tax levied on goods at the point of importation into a country, paid by the importer.

**Terms of trade** — the ratio of a country's export prices to its import prices. An improvement in the terms of trade means exports buy more imports; a deterioration means exports buy fewer imports.

**Value of exports** — the total revenue from exports, equal to the price of exports multiplied by the volume (quantity) exported. A fall in export prices does not necessarily reduce the value of exports if the volume increases sufficiently; the net effect depends on the price elasticity of demand.

**World Trade Organisation (WTO)** — the international organisation that oversees the rules of global trade, provides a forum for trade negotiations, and operates the Dispute Settlement Body. Has 164 member countries.

## 8. Exam Technique — Consolidated Reminders

The technique callouts throughout these notes are the most important part of the document for exam preparation. Here they are gathered together for reference:

**On diagram questions (9-mark):**
Do not evaluate. Spend every sentence explaining mechanism. Label axes every time — "Price (£)" on the vertical, "Quantity" on the horizontal. Name every area and explain what it means economically, not just geometrically. Practice drawing the diagram in under 90 seconds so you can spend the rest of the time explaining it.

**On "value vs volume":**
The value of exports = price × volume. A depreciation raises the domestic-currency price of imports and lowers the foreign-currency price of exports. Whether the value of exports rises or falls depends on the price elasticity of demand for exports. Never write "exports become more expensive so export revenue rises" — it depends on elasticity.

**On expenditure-switching vs expenditure-reducing:**
Expenditure-switching redirects spending from imports to domestic goods (tariff, depreciation). Expenditure-reducing lowers total spending (higher interest rates, fiscal austerity). They have different macroeconomic consequences. Do not conflate them.

**On current account deficit vs government budget deficit:**
These are distinct. The current account deficit is the gap between national spending on imports and earnings from exports. The budget deficit is the gap between government spending and tax revenue. They can move in opposite directions. Confusion between them is one of the most heavily flagged errors in examiner reports.

**On essay structure for 25-mark questions:**
The top band requires evaluation *throughout*, not just in a final paragraph. After making each argument, qualify it: specify the conditions under which it holds and the conditions under which it does not. Draw those conditions together into a conclusion that makes a specific judgement — not a balanced "on one hand, on the other hand" non-conclusion, but a reasoned verdict.

**On use of real-world examples:**
Current affairs knowledge is explicitly rewarded in the top band of 25-mark questions. The US-China trade war, post-Brexit trade policy, and the EU Common External Tariff are all relevant to protectionism questions. Use specific data where you know it (e.g. "US tariffs escalated to cover over $350 billion of Chinese goods by 2019"), but a correctly-analysed example without precise numbers is better than a precise number deployed without analysis.

**On choosing which data to use in data-response questions:**
Choose data points that let you develop an argument, not just illustrate a trend. The examiner rewards analytical use of data — explaining what a statistic implies about an economic relationship — not quotation. If you use a figure, explain what it tells us about the economy, not just what it shows.
