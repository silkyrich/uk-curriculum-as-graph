Protectionism: Why Countries Build Walls Around Their Economies

In 2018, President Trump imposed 25% tariffs on imported steel. Within weeks, the EU, Canada, and China had all retaliated. American soybean farmers — who had no quarrel with China — lost their primary export market. This unit explains how that sequence works, and whether it ever makes economic sense.

What is Free Trade?

A quick recap of comparative advantage and the gains from trade — the benchmark against which protectionism is measured.

Speaker Notes — Slide 3 of 38

This section assumes students have already covered comparative advantage. Do not re-teach it. The goal is a 5-minute mental reset: remind them what the world looks like without protection, so they have a baseline for understanding what protection does. If students look blank at 'comparative advantage', do a 30-second verbal recap: 'Countries should produce what they're relatively best at, and trade for the rest — even if one country is better at everything.' Then move on.

Free Trade: The Baseline

  • AO1Comparative advantage: countries specialise in goods where their relative efficiency is highest and trade for the rest
  • Even if one country is absolutely better at producing everything, both countries gain from specialisation and trade
  • AO1Free trade increases total global output and the size of the economic pie
  • AO2Gains from trade: economies of scale (larger markets), lower prices for consumers, greater variety, competitive discipline on domestic firms
  • AO3The political problem: gains are diffuse (many consumers gain a little), losses are concentrated (specific workers and firms lose a lot)
  • Protectionism is the systematic response to that political asymmetry — not to the economics
Key question: If free trade increases total welfare, why does protectionism persist? Answer: because the losers from free trade vote, donate, and lobby — and the winners barely notice their gain.
Speaker Notes — Slide 4 of 38

The political economy point (bullet 5) is the single most useful framing for the entire unit and for AO4 evaluation. Spend 60 seconds on it. 'Why does protectionism persist even when economists overwhelmingly support free trade?' Expect answers like 'jobs' or 'national security' — validate these but redirect to the political asymmetry. A steelworker who loses their job knows exactly who to blame. The millions of consumers who each save £1 on a fridge never organise into a lobby. This asymmetry drives protectionist policy in all democracies. Timing: 5 minutes including image reference.

The Instruments of Protectionism

Tariffs, quotas, and export subsidies — how each works and what makes them different from one another.

Speaker Notes — Slide 5 of 38

Before this section, put the following on the board and leave it up: 'Tariff = tax on imports. Quota = physical limit on import quantity. Export subsidy = government payment per unit exported.' Students will see these again in more depth, but having the bare definitions visible helps anchor the analysis that follows. This section includes the most examined piece of content in the unit: the tariff diagram.

Three Instruments of Protectionism

  • AO1TARIFF: a tax on imported goods at point of entry — raises the domestic price above the world price
  • Ad valorem tariff: expressed as % of import price (e.g. 25% on steel)
  • Specific tariff: fixed monetary amount per unit (e.g. £50 per tonne)
  • AO1QUOTA: a direct quantitative limit on import volumes — price adjusts, not quantity
  • AO2Key distinction: quota generates no government revenue (quota rent accrues to licence holders)
  • AO1EXPORT SUBSIDY: government payment per unit exported — allows domestic firms to undercut foreign rivals in export markets
  • AO2Cost borne by the exporting country's taxpayers; classified as prohibited subsidy under WTO rules
  • Examiner alert: students who conflate these three instruments are answering at the lowest level-of-response band
The critical distinction: tariff = government revenue. Quota = quota rent to licence holders. Export subsidy = cost to government. These are not interchangeable.
Speaker Notes — Slide 6 of 38

Take each instrument in turn. For tariffs: ask 'who pays?' (importer, passed on to consumer). For quotas: ask 'where does the money go that would have been tariff revenue?' (quota rent — often to foreign exporters who hold licences). This is a subtle point that most students miss. For export subsidies: the EU's Common Agricultural Policy is the standard example — EU farmers could sell grain below world price because taxpayers subsidised every tonne exported, devastating developing-country farmers who couldn't compete. Timing: 8 minutes on this slide.

The Tariff Diagram — Step by Step

⚠ EXAMINER ERRORS — flag these explicitly: (1) Axes left unlabelled — automatic deduction; (2) Drawing S_d shifting left instead of a new price line — wrong model entirely; (3) Q3 and Q4 reversed — supply increases and demand decreases simultaneously; (4) Revenue rectangle drawn but B and D triangles omitted — incomplete welfare analysis; (5) Describing the diagram ('price rises, imports fall') rather than explaining mechanism ('because the tariff raises the domestic price by the amount of the tax, domestic producers face a higher effective price and expand output along the existing supply curve').
  1. Step 1: Step 1 — AXES. Draw vertical axis labelled 'Price (£)' — not just P. Draw horizontal axis labelled 'Quantity' — not just Q. Unlabelled axes lose marks automatically.
  2. Step 2: Step 2 — CURVES. Draw upward-sloping domestic supply curve S_d and downward-sloping demand curve D. Do NOT show their intersection as a point yet — the world price sits below it.
  3. Step 3: Step 3 — WORLD PRICE LINE. Draw horizontal line Pw below the domestic equilibrium intersection. This represents the small-open-economy assumption: the country is a price-taker and cannot affect the world price.
  4. Step 4: Step 4 — FREE TRADE QUANTITIES. At Pw: read off Q1 (domestic supply — where S_d meets Pw) and Q2 (total demand — where D meets Pw). Imports under free trade = Q2 minus Q1. Mark Q1 and Q2 on the X-axis with dotted vertical lines.
  5. Step 5: Step 5 — TARIFF LINE. Draw second horizontal line Pw+t above Pw. Vertical distance between the two lines = tariff per unit. Label it Pw+tariff or Pw+t.
  6. Step 6: Step 6 — POST-TARIFF QUANTITIES. At Pw+t: Q3 = new domestic supply (higher price draws more domestic production — Q3 > Q1). Q4 = new domestic demand (higher price contracts demand — Q4 < Q2). X-axis order left to right: Q1, Q3, Q4, Q2. New imports = Q4 minus Q3.
  7. Step 7: Step 7 — WELFARE AREAS. Label four areas: A = producer surplus gain (left of C, between Pw and Pw+t, left boundary = Q1). B = deadweight loss triangle (production inefficiency — small triangle between Q1 and Q3, below S_d). C = government revenue rectangle (between Q3 and Q4, between Pw and Pw+t). D = deadweight loss triangle (consumption reduction — small triangle between Q4 and Q2, above D curve).
  8. Step 8: Step 8 — NARRATE. Consumer surplus loss = A+B+C+D. Producer gain = A. Government revenue = C. Deadweight losses (permanently destroyed) = B+D.

This is the most important slide in the deck. Budget 20-25 minutes across two phases: (1) live draw with narration — draw each step as you say it, students copy simultaneously — never hand out a completed diagram; (2) common errors debrief with a deliberately wrong diagram. DRAW SEQUENCE: Start with axes. Say 'Price in pounds on the vertical — always in pounds, never just P.' Draw S_d and D. Say 'I am NOT drawing the equilibrium point yet — watch why.' Draw Pw well below the intersection. Say 'At the world price, what do domestic producers supply? Find where S_d meets Pw — that's Q1. What does domestic demand look like at this price? Find where D meets Pw — that's Q2. The gap is our imports under free trade.' Then introduce the tariff line. Slow down at Step 7 — many students draw only the rectangle C and stop. Say explicitly: 'The rectangle is not enough. You need both triangles. B is left of C — it's the cost of drawing domestic resources into production at a cost above the world price. D is right of C — it's the consumption that has been killed off by the price rise. These triangles are welfare destroyed, transferred to nobody.' After the draw: show a wrong version (S_d shifting, Q3 and Q4 swapped). Ask students to identify the error. 90 seconds for students to redraw from memory.

Tariff: Worked Numerical Example

  1. Given: World price Pw = £10. Government imposes tariff of £4 per unit. New domestic price = £14.
  2. Free trade (at £10): Domestic supply Q1 = 100 units. Domestic demand Q2 = 300 units. Imports = Q2 - Q1 = 200 units.
  3. Post-tariff (at £14): Domestic supply Q3 = 140 units. Domestic demand Q4 = 260 units. Imports = Q4 - Q3 = 120 units. Import volume has fallen by 80 units (40%).
  4. Government revenue = tariff per unit x post-tariff import volume = £4 x 120 = £480
  5. Producer surplus gain (approximate, assuming linear supply) = £4 x average of (100 + 140) / 2 = £4 x 120 = £480
  6. Consumer surplus loss (approximate, assuming linear demand) = £4 x average of (300 + 260) / 2 = £4 x 280 = £1,120
  7. Deadweight loss = Consumer surplus loss - Producer surplus gain - Government revenue = £1,120 - £480 - £480 = £160
  8. Check: DWL = area B + area D = two triangles. This £160 is welfare destroyed and transferred to nobody.
Exam tip: Always show working. Always include units (£, 'units'). A correct method with a minor arithmetic error scores more marks than a correct answer with no working. State what each number represents — do not just write '£480' without labelling it 'government revenue = tariff x import volume'.
Speaker Notes — Slide 8 of 38

Work through this at the board. Do not rush. Ask students to predict each answer before you reveal it: 'If the tariff is £4 and 120 units are still imported, what is government revenue?' Wait for hands. The calculation structure is: revenue = tariff x remaining imports; producer gain = tariff x average of old and new domestic supply; consumer loss = tariff x average of old and new demand. Deadweight loss is what's left over. Key pedagogical point: note that producer gain (£480) equals government revenue (£480) in this example — a coincidence of the numbers, not a general rule. The consumer loss (£1,120) is more than twice either. That asymmetry is the welfare story. Timing: 8 minutes.

Quotas: The Alternative to Tariffs

  • AO1A quota is a direct quantitative limit on import volumes — where a tariff raises price and lets quantity adjust, a quota fixes quantity and lets price adjust
  • AO2Price effect: domestic price still rises above world price (just as with a tariff) — consumers are equally harmed in direction
  • AO2Critical difference: NO government revenue
  • AO2Quota rent: the value equivalent to area C in the tariff diagram accrues to whoever holds import licences — typically foreign exporters or lucky importers
  • AO3Quotas are less transparent than tariffs: harder to measure, harder to negotiate down in trade rounds
  • AO3WTO position: GATT Article XI generally prohibits quotas; WTO has pushed for 'tariffication' — converting quotas to equivalent tariffs — because tariffs are more visible and negotiable
  • Unpredictability: if demand shifts, the domestic price impact of a quota is harder to control than with a tariff
Exam trap: a student who knows tariff = revenue, quota = no revenue, and can explain quota rent is answering in the top two response bands. Most students don't know what quota rent is.
Speaker Notes — Slide 9 of 38

The quota rent concept is the differentiator for high-band students. Draw the parallel: 'In the tariff diagram, area C was government revenue. Now imagine we capped imports at the same level by quota instead of tariff. Area C still exists — someone gains from selling at the higher domestic price. But who? The importer who got lucky with a licence, or the foreign exporter who negotiated one. The government gets nothing.' Pause and ask: 'Is that better or worse than a tariff from the UK government's perspective?' (Worse — revenue foregone.) Timing: 6 minutes.

Export Subsidies: Paying to Compete Abroad

  • AO1An export subsidy is a government payment to domestic producers per unit exported — allows them to sell in foreign markets below their cost of production
  • AO2Who benefits: domestic producers (higher effective price = market price + subsidy). Foreign consumers (lower prices in short run)
  • AO2Who loses: the exporting country's taxpayers. Domestic producers in the importing country who cannot compete with artificially cheap imports
  • AO3Historical example: EU Common Agricultural Policy — export subsidies allowed European farmers to sell grain, butter, and sugar below world prices, depressing global commodity prices and harming developing-country farmers
  • AO2WTO classification: 'prohibited subsidy' — the most serious category. Countries can challenge them and apply countervailing duties if upheld
  • AO3EU formally ended most agricultural export subsidies in 2013 under WTO pressure — though domestic production subsidies remain
  • AO3Current example: EU anti-dumping tariffs of up to 47% on Chinese electric vehicles (2024)
Development dimension: WTO estimates EU and US agricultural subsidies cost developing-country exporters over $50 billion per year in lost earnings — more than total aid received.
Speaker Notes — Slide 10 of 38

The development dimension callout is high-value AO3/AO4 material for 25-mark essays. Ask: 'If I'm a Ghanaian cocoa farmer and the EU subsidises European chocolate exports, what happens to the world price for my cocoa?' (It falls.) 'Can I compete?' (No.) This connects protectionism in rich countries to development outcomes in poor ones. Timing: 6 minutes.

Expenditure-Switching vs Expenditure-Reducing

  • AO1EXPENDITURE-SWITCHING: policies that redirect domestic spending from foreign to domestic goods by changing relative prices — total spending broadly unchanged
  • Tools: tariffs, import quotas, exchange rate depreciation, voluntary export restraints
  • Key word: REDIRECTION — same wallet, different direction
  • AO1EXPENDITURE-REDUCING: policies that reduce total aggregate demand — total spending falls, dragging imports down with everything else
  • Tools: deflationary fiscal policy (tax rises, spending cuts), higher interest rates
  • Key word: REDUCTION — smaller wallet overall
  • Critical examiner error: classifying exchange rate depreciation as expenditure-reducing. Depreciation changes relative prices — it is expenditure-switching.
  • AO4Note: a tariff can have both effects simultaneously — primarily switching, but if widespread, may reduce real income and total spending
Household analogy: switching = stop buying French wine, buy English wine instead (same spend, different destination). Reducing = stop buying wine altogether (total spend falls).
Speaker Notes — Slide 11 of 38

The expenditure-switching / expenditure-reducing confusion is flagged across multiple examiner reports as a recurring error. Teach it with the household analogy first. Then connect to national economy. Leave the two-column table on the board. Students copy. Then give 5 verbal examples and ask students to categorise in pairs. Mark immediately. Timing: 8 minutes.

Why Do Countries Use Protection?

The arguments for protectionism — and how to deploy them as structured analytical paragraphs, not bullet-point lists.

Speaker Notes — Slide 12 of 38

Explicitly brief students before this section: 'I am going to teach you these arguments as exam paragraphs. Not as bullet points. Each one needs: claim, mechanism, evidence, qualification. If you write these as a list in a 25-mark essay, you will cap out at Level 3.' This framing changes how students listen to the next two slides.

Arguments For and Against Protectionism

Arguments FOR (with conditions)

  • Infant industry: temporary protection until economies of scale achieved and comparative advantage established — requires capital market failure and credible exit rule
  • Strategic industries / national security: food, energy, defence, semiconductors — COVID-19 exposed supply chain dependency on imports for PPE and pharmaceuticals
  • Anti-dumping: WTO permits tariffs where foreign producers sell below cost to eliminate domestic competition — US steel tariffs on China; EU EV tariffs 2024
  • Rebalancing current account deficit: expenditure-switching effect redirects demand to domestic goods
  • Protecting employment: short-run jobs preserved in import-competing industries — politically salient even if economically contested
  • Terms of trade (large country only): tariff may improve terms of trade if country has market power — limited applicability to UK

Arguments AGAINST / Costs

  • Retaliation: trading partners impose counter-tariffs — UK's export industries (financial services, pharmaceuticals, aerospace) are more exposed than import-competing ones
  • Consumer welfare loss: tariff is a consumption tax paid by domestic consumers — regressive (lower-income households spend more of income on traded goods)
  • Productive inefficiency: protected firms face reduced competition — lower incentive to innovate, X-inefficiency, long-run competitiveness worsens
  • WTO dispute risk: post-Brexit UK is standalone WTO member — breaching bound tariff rates invites proceedings
  • Resource misallocation: factors of production kept in low-productivity protected industries rather than moving to comparative advantage sectors
  • Global welfare falls: deadweight losses accumulate; protectionist spiral risk — Smoot-Hawley (1930) collapsed world trade 65% in four years
Speaker Notes — Slide 13 of 38

Use this two-column layout to set up the essay structure conversation. Say: 'Notice that the strongest counterargument to the infant industry case is not just consumer welfare loss — it's that governments have poor information about which industries have genuine long-run comparative advantage. That's a Paper 1 government failure argument embedded in a Paper 2 context. That's the analytical move that reaches Level 5.' For retaliation: use the US-China trade war. For consumer welfare: note the regressive distributional effect. Timing: 8 minutes.

The Infant Industry Argument — In Depth

  • AO1Core claim: a new domestic industry cannot survive foreign competition before achieving economies of scale and moving down the learning curve — temporary protection gives it space to develop
  • AO3Evidence it can work: South Korea in 1960s–80s — targeted protection + state support built POSCO (steel), Samsung (semiconductors), and shipbuilding into world-competitive industries
  • Required conditions (each is a point of failure):
  • AO4Government can identify industries with genuine long-run comparative advantage (information problem — markets may handle this better than bureaucrats)
  • AO4Protection is genuinely temporary — exit mechanism exists and is credible (political economy works against exit: once an industry employs workers and donates politically, protection becomes permanent)
  • AO4Capital market failure prevents private financing — if future profits are large enough, a firm should be able to borrow to survive the loss-making early years
  • AO4Net present value of protection period costs must be less than future competitive gains
Evaluation structure for 25-mark essays: 'The infant industry argument is compelling WHEN capital markets are failing AND government can identify a viable industry — but less compelling when politicians protect incumbents to preserve votes.'
Speaker Notes — Slide 14 of 38

This is the most intellectually substantive argument for protection and the one examiners most reward when analysed with precision. The South Korea example is genuine — Samsung and POSCO are now world leaders. But the Brazil computer industry example cuts the other way: Brazil tried to protect its domestic computing industry in the 1980s and produced a decade of artificially expensive, uncompetitive computers. The difference lies in the conditions. The government failure point (condition 1) connects to Paper 1 — a synoptic link that examiners specifically value. Timing: 10 minutes including student task to write one evaluation sentence.

Terms of Trade and Trade Creation vs Diversion

  • AO1Terms of trade = ratio of export prices to import prices. An improvement means more imports per unit of exports.
  • AO3Large-country tariff argument: if a country has market power (large share of world demand), imposing a tariff may cause foreign exporters to lower their prices to maintain market access — part of the tariff is borne by the exporter
  • AO3Effect: terms of trade improve. The country imports at a lower effective price. Welfare may increase net of the DWL.
  • AO4Caveat: UK is a small open economy in most goods markets — this argument has very limited applicability to UK policy
  • A* stretch: optimal tariff = rate that maximises national welfare (terms of trade gain minus DWL). A prohibitive tariff destroys trade entirely — not optimal.
Speaker Notes — Slide 15 of 38

Present the terms of trade argument as the 'economist's case for protectionism' — valid under specific conditions, rarely applicable to the UK. Ask: 'Why does this argument almost never appear in UK policy debates?' (UK does not have sufficient market power in most goods — it cannot affect world prices.) The A* stretch is optional — use it only if the group is ahead of pace. Timing: 4 minutes.

The World in Practice

Current real-world cases: US-China trade war, post-Brexit UK trade policy, and the WTO's role (and limits).

Speaker Notes — Slide 16 of 38

Brief students: 'Top-band 25-mark essays require contextual grounding in specific examples. You need to know more than the name — you need the mechanism. For each case study, I will give you: what happened, what the stated reason was, and what the economic effect actually was. That gap between stated reason and actual effect is the source of your best evaluation points.' Aim to cover all three cases in approximately 15 minutes combined.

Customs Unions — AQA Requirement

  • Four levels of regional integration — know the distinctions precisely:
  • AO1Free Trade Area: internal tariffs removed; no CET; members negotiate own external trade deals (e.g. CPTPP)
  • AO1Customs Union: internal tariffs removed + common external tariff (CET); members cannot negotiate independent deals
  • AO1Single Market: customs union + free movement of goods, services, capital, and labour + regulatory harmonisation
  • AO1Currency Union: single market + shared currency (eurozone — subset of EU members)
  • AO2Trade creation: replacing higher-cost domestic production with lower-cost partner imports — welfare gain
  • AO2Trade diversion: replacing lower-cost non-member imports with higher-cost partner imports due to the CET — welfare loss
  • AO3Net welfare effect of joining a customs union = trade creation minus trade diversion
  • AO3Post-Brexit: UK left EU customs union — now operates as a free trade area partner via TCA (zero tariffs on goods meeting rules of origin, but non-tariff barriers apply)
Speaker Notes — Slide 17 of 38

The four-structure table is worth five minutes of board time. Build it row by row, asking students to identify which additional feature each level adds. Then use Brexit as the application: 'Where did the UK sit before January 2021? Single market + customs union. Where does it sit now? Effectively a free trade area with additional non-tariff barriers.' The trade creation/diversion distinction is the key discriminator for high-band answers on customs union questions. Most students know trade creation but not trade diversion. Timing: 8 minutes.

US-China Trade War: 2018–Present

  • AO32018: Trump administration imposed 25% tariffs on $34 billion of Chinese goods. Stated reason: unfair trade practices — state subsidies, forced tech transfer, IP theft
  • AO3Escalation: by 2019, tariffs covered over $350 billion of Chinese imports
  • AO3China retaliated: tariffs on US agricultural exports — soybean farmers, pork producers, lobster exporters bore the cost
  • AO3Biden administration: maintained tariffs; 2024 raised tariffs on Chinese EVs to 100%, solar cells to 50%, semiconductors to 50%
  • AO4What actually happened: US trade deficit with China widened in some years; supply chains rerouted via Vietnam/Mexico rather than dismantled; American consumers paid higher prices
  • AO4Chinese response to semiconductor tariffs: accelerated domestic investment — arguably strengthening rather than weakening Chinese technological capacity
  • AO3IMF estimate: the conflict reduced global output by around 0.5% of GDP
Use this as evaluation evidence: 'The US-China trade war demonstrates that tariffs do not necessarily achieve their stated industrial policy goals — the deficit widened, jobs were not reshored, and US firms dependent on Chinese inputs saw costs rise.'
Speaker Notes — Slide 18 of 38

Ask students: 'What was the stated purpose of the US steel tariffs?' (protect American steel jobs, national security). 'Did they achieve it?' (steel employment did rise slightly, but steel-using manufacturers — 80x more employees — faced higher input costs). 'What happened to the trade deficit?' (widened). This gap between intention and outcome is the basis for strong AO4 evaluation. The soybean farmer point is rhetorically powerful: Chinese retaliation targeted US agricultural exports precisely because American swing states produce soybeans. Timing: 6 minutes.

Post-Brexit UK Trade Policy

  • AO1Before 2021: UK trade policy set by the EU — applied Common External Tariff (averaging 5–6% overall), zero tariffs within Single Market
  • AO2Post-Brexit UK Global Tariff: broadly replicates CET but with unilateral reductions — zero tariffs on many food and consumer products
  • AO2UK-EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA): zero tariffs on goods meeting rules of origin — but NOT a customs union. Regulatory divergence + border checks = significant non-tariff barriers
  • AO3Estimated impact: non-tariff barriers from Brexit have reduced UK-EU goods trade by 15–25% vs the counterfactual
  • AO3Services excluded from TCA: UK financial services firms lost EU passporting rights — a serious loss given UK comparative advantage in services
  • AO4UK joined CPTPP (Trans-Pacific Partnership): genuine liberalisation move — but UK-EU trade is 50%+ of exports and dwarfs these deals
  • AO4FTA negotiations with India and others: take 3–7 years; trade-offs are political as much as economic
Essay hook: 'Post-Brexit, the UK can set its own trade policy — but the UK-EU TCA demonstrates that non-tariff barriers can be as restrictive as tariffs themselves, without generating any government revenue.'
Speaker Notes — Slide 19 of 38

The key analytical point for AO4 essays: Brexit allowed the UK to set its own tariffs — a genuine policy freedom — but the non-tariff barriers introduced with the EU are arguably more trade-restricting than the tariffs ever were. The services point (passporting) is important: the UK's actual comparative advantage is in financial services, not in goods. Timing: 6 minutes.

The WTO: Role, Principles, and Limits

  • AO1WTO: 164 member countries. Promotes multilateral trade liberalisation through negotiating rounds (Doha Round stalled)
  • AO1Most Favoured Nation (MFN) principle: WTO members must apply the same tariff rate to all other WTO members — cannot discriminate
  • AO1Binding of tariffs: members agree maximum bound rates and cannot raise tariffs above them unilaterally
  • AO2WTO Dispute Settlement Body (DSB): quasi-judicial mechanism — panel hears complaints, rules on violations, authorises retaliation if not remedied
  • AO2The WTO does NOT prohibit all tariffs. Permitted exceptions: anti-dumping measures, national security (Article XXI), infant industry provisions for developing nations
  • AO3Appellate Body paralysed since 2019: US has blocked appointment of new judges — appeals cannot be heard, significantly weakening enforcement
  • AO3Case example: Airbus-Boeing subsidies dispute — 17 years, both EU and US found to have illegally subsidised aircraft manufacturers, settled 2021
Common misconception: the WTO does not ban tariffs — it binds their level and prohibits discrimination. Post-Brexit, the UK is a standalone WTO member.
Speaker Notes — Slide 20 of 38

Students often describe the WTO as 'stopping tariffs' — correct this immediately. The WTO constrains the level of tariffs (bound rates), prohibits discrimination (MFN), and provides dispute resolution. It does not prevent countries from having tariffs. The Appellate Body paralysis point is genuinely important for current affairs essays. Timing: 5 minutes.

The $7,500 price rise per car is tangible — 'A US consumer buying a German BMW pays $7,500 more.' The UK Angle: Inside the Deal

  • AO3UK-US Economic Prosperity Deal (May 2025): 100,000 UK vehicles exported to US at 10% tariff — not the standard 25%
  • Above 100,000 vehicles, the rate reverts to 25% — this is a quota in all but name
  • AO3UK steel: 25% tariff under Section 232 — versus 50% for all other countries — a partial exemption
  • JLR (Jaguar Land Rover): paused US-bound shipments on Liberation Day, then resumed after the deal — 22bn USD US investment commitment used as negotiating leverage
  • AO3MFN problem: WTO Most Favoured Nation principle requires any advantage given to one country to be extended to all. How does the UK justify 10% while the EU pays 20%?
  • Possible legal basis: Article XXIV FTA exemption — but no full FTA was signed. A selective exemption without an FTA is legally exposed to WTO challenge.

The MFN bullet is the killer exam application. 'Theory says the WTO prevents discrimination — yet the UK got a better deal than the EU. Is this WTO-compliant?' FOR: part of a broader USTK FTA framework. AGAINST: selective sector carve-out with no FTA signed. This is Band 3 to Band 4 territory. The JLR detail is memorable evidence: a student who writes 'the UK leveraged a 22bn USD investment commitment to negotiate a tariff exemption for car exports' is demonstrating AO3 analysis with specific evidence.

Examiner Error #1: Describing the Diagram Instead of Explaining It

✗ Common error
Students write what the tariff diagram looks like — 'the price rises, imports fall, consumer surplus decreases' — without explaining why each change occurs or what it means economically.
✓ Correct approach
Every diagrammatic change needs a causal chain. The mark scheme rewards process, not description. For every feature you label, write one sentence that begins with 'because' or 'this means that'. Example: 'Consumer surplus falls by areas A+B+C+D BECAUSE the higher tariff-inclusive price reduces the quantity demanded along the demand curve, and some consumers who were willing to pay above the world price but below the domestic tariff price no longer purchase the good — their surplus is eliminated entirely.'
Examiner report: “Weaker responses tended to describe the diagram — stating that price rose and imports fell — rather than explaining the economic mechanism. Credit was only awarded where candidates explained the causal process connecting the tariff to each welfare effect.” — AQA Economics Paper 2 Examiner Report Jun 2022
Speaker Notes — Slide 31 of 38

Read the examiner quote aloud. Ask students: 'What is the difference between saying the price rises and explaining why the price rises?' Give 30 seconds. Then demonstrate: write on the board — 'The domestic price rises.' Now extend it: '...because the tariff is a tax on every imported unit, passed through by the importer to the consumer as a higher retail price.' That sentence is worth AO3 marks. The first sentence is worth AO2 at best. Timing: 4 minutes.

Examiner Error #2: Confusing the Current Account Deficit with the Budget Deficit

✗ Common error
Students answer questions about the current account deficit — an international trade imbalance — by writing about government borrowing or fiscal policy. The two are conflated because both are called 'deficits'.
✓ Correct approach
Write both definitions on the board simultaneously and keep them visible: CURRENT ACCOUNT DEFICIT = national spending on imports > earnings from exports (international trade balance). BUDGET (FISCAL) DEFICIT = government spending > tax revenue (public finance). They have different causes, different policy responses, and can move in opposite directions.
Examiner report: “A significant minority of candidates confused the current account deficit with the government budget deficit, deploying fiscal policy analysis where trade analysis was required. This error was present across all ability bands and caused substantial mark loss.” — AQA Economics Paper 2 Examiner Report Jun 2023
Speaker Notes — Slide 32 of 38

This error appears across all ability bands — not just weaker students. Write the two definitions side by side on the board and leave them visible for the rest of the session. Ask a student to state, in their own words, the difference. The expenditure-switching vs expenditure-reducing confusion is a related error. Teach them with a household analogy: switching = stop buying French wine, buy English wine instead; reducing = stop buying wine altogether. Timing: 4 minutes.

Examiner Error #3: Confusing Currency Union with Customs Union

✗ Common error
Students answer questions about customs unions by writing about currency unions (shared currency, monetary policy), or conflate free trade areas with customs unions.
✓ Correct approach
Customs union = internal free trade + common external tariff. Currency union = customs union + single market + shared currency. These are structurally and mechanically different. Brexit moved the UK from Single Market membership (which includes customs union) to a Free Trade Area (TCA) — zero tariffs but no CET, no free movement, non-tariff barriers apply.
Examiner report: “Responses to the customs union question frequently described features of a monetary union (shared currency, common interest rates) rather than the defining features of a customs union. This is a fundamental category error.” — AQA Economics Paper 2 Examiner Report Jun 2023
Speaker Notes — Slide 33 of 38

Show the four-structure table again. Ask students to reproduce it from memory before showing it. Give 90 seconds. Score by show of hands: who got all four rows? This is a table worth 5+ marks across an exam series. It must be automatic. Timing: 3 minutes.

25-Mark Essay Structure

  • Introduction (2–3 sentences): define key term, contextualise (UK post-Brexit, WTO, current tensions). Do NOT evaluate in the introduction.
  • Paragraph 1 — Argument + Analysis + INTEGRATED evaluation: state argument → analyse mechanism → immediately evaluate: 'However, this depends on...' + UK context
  • Paragraphs 2–3: repeat structure. Each paragraph must contain at least one evaluative sentence — not saved for the end.
  • Paragraph 4 — Counterargument: lead with the strongest opposing argument. Evaluate its limits if credible.
  • Conclusion: a genuine conditional judgement. Not 'both sides have valid points.' Specify WHEN protectionism is justified and WHEN it is not.
  • AQA Level 5 (22–25 marks) requires: 'Sustained and well-developed evaluation applied THROUGHOUT. Supported judgements in context. Reasoned conclusion.'
The most productive feedback signal: mark where in the essay evaluation FIRST appears. If not present until the final paragraph, the student cannot reach Level 5 regardless of knowledge quality.
Speaker Notes — Slide 34 of 38

Brief students before they start the 25-mark practice: 'In 25-mark questions, the examiner cannot give you Level 5 if evaluation only appears in the final paragraph. The structural rule is: evaluate as you argue. Every paragraph should contain the argument AND its qualification.' Give 5 minutes for planning. Timing: 3 minutes on this slide.

Key Takeaways: Protectionism (3.2.6.2)

  • THREE INSTRUMENTS: Tariff (tax, generates revenue), Quota (quantity limit, no revenue — quota rent), Export subsidy (payment per unit exported, cost to taxpayer, WTO-prohibited)
  • TARIFF DIAGRAM: non-negotiable. Axes labelled Price (£) and Quantity. Welfare areas A (producer gain), B (DWL production), C (government revenue), D (DWL consumption). Consumer surplus loss = A+B+C+D. Net welfare loss = B+D.
  • EXPENDITURE-SWITCHING vs EXPENDITURE-REDUCING: switching changes composition of spending (tariff, depreciation). Reducing lowers total spending (interest rates, fiscal austerity).
  • CURRENT ACCOUNT DEFICIT vs BUDGET DEFICIT: completely different. Current account = trade balance. Budget = fiscal balance. Confusion costs marks in every exam series.
  • INFANT INDUSTRY: valid under specific conditions (capital market failure, credible exit, correct government identification). Fails on information problem and political economy of exit.
  • WTO: does NOT ban tariffs — binds their level (MFN principle, bound rates), prohibits discrimination. Appellate Body paralysed since 2019.
  • UK POST-BREXIT: standalone WTO member, own Global Tariff, TCA with EU (zero tariffs but non-tariff barriers). Services excluded. CPTPP joined. UK-EU trade = 50%+ of exports.
  • 25-MARK ESSAYS: evaluation must be THROUGHOUT, not just in final paragraph. Conclusions must be specific and conditional. UK context required in every paragraph.
