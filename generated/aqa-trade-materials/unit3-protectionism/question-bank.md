## Section 1: Free Trade and Comparative Advantage

### Mark Scheme: Q1 [2 marks] Knowledge Check

*Question ID: `S01-Q01`*

Award 1 mark for correct method: opportunity cost of cloth = wheat foregone / cloth produced = 20 ÷ 10. Award second mark for: 2 units of wheat (correct answer with correct units). Do not accept 0.5 (this is the opportunity cost of wheat in terms of cloth — a common reversal error). Any answer without units earns maximum 1 mark.

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Inverting the ratio — calculating opportunity cost of wheat instead of cloth. The question specifies 'opportunity cost of cloth' so the answer must be expressed in units of wheat given up.

## Section 3: Quotas and Export Subsidies

### Mark Scheme: Q7 [2 marks] Knowledge Check

*Question ID: `S03-Q01`*

Award 1 mark for: a limit / restriction on the quantity (or volume) of imports. Award second mark for any of: set by the government; reduces supply of imports into the domestic market; raises the domestic market price; a form of non-tariff barrier / protectionism. Do not accept: 'a limit on the price of imports' (confuses quota with price control). 'A limit on exports' (must specify imports in protectionism context).

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Conflating quota with tariff — students define a quota as 'a tax on imports'. A quota is a quantity limit, not a tax.


### Mark Scheme: Q8 [2 marks] Knowledge Check

*Question ID: `S03-Q02`*

Award 1 mark for: a payment (by the government) to domestic producers to reduce their costs of production / lower the price of exported goods. Award second mark for any of: makes domestic goods cheaper in foreign markets; increases the quantity of exports; a form of protectionism; funded from government revenue. Do not accept: 'a tax on exports' (fundamental confusion — a subsidy is a payment). 'Makes imports cheaper' (export subsidy affects export prices, not import prices).

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Confusing export subsidies with import subsidies or general production subsidies. The export context is essential.


### Mark Scheme: Q9 [9 marks] Analysis

*Question ID: `S03-Q03`*

Level 1 (1–3 marks): knows a quota restricts imports but analysis is superficial. Level 2 (4–6 marks): correctly identifies quota reduces import supply raising domestic price; domestic producers benefit; some consumer impact understood; quota rent issue or efficiency effects missing. Level 3 (7–9 marks): Chain 1 — quota limits import supply → domestic price rises → domestic industry expands output → higher revenue/profits → labour demand rises → BUT productive efficiency may fall over time. Chain 2 — consumer surplus falls, some consumers priced out, downstream industries face higher input costs. Chain 3 — quota rent: unlike tariff, government collects no revenue; price wedge accrues to licence holder (typically foreign exporter) — transfer from domestic consumers to foreign firms with no domestic benefit. Chain 4 — domestic resources drawn into production above world price — production efficiency loss.

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Treating quotas and tariffs as having identical effects in all respects. The quota rent distinction is the key discriminator between Level 2 and Level 3.


### Mark Scheme: Q10 [9 marks] Analysis

*Question ID: `S03-Q04`*

Level 1 (1–3 marks): knows current account deficit needs correcting but cannot define or distinguish the two policy types. Level 2 (4–6 marks): correctly defines or characterises at least one category; gives at least one accurate example per category; some understanding of mechanism. Level 3 (7–9 marks): Expenditure-switching — definition: policies that divert domestic spending from imports to domestically produced goods without necessarily reducing total spending; mechanism: change relative prices; examples: tariffs, quotas, exchange rate depreciation; effect: import volume falls, exports more competitive. Expenditure-reducing — definition: reduce total domestic expenditure; mechanism: reduce national income/AD; examples: deflationary fiscal policy (tax rises, spending cuts), higher interest rates; effect: lower income → lower spending on imports (but total output falls too). Key distinction must be explicit: switching targets composition of spending; reducing targets the level of total spending — different transmission mechanisms, different side-effects for growth and employment.

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Classifying exchange rate depreciation as expenditure-reducing. Depreciation changes relative prices — it is expenditure-switching. This is the most common error in this topic.

## Section 5: The Global Trading System

### Mark Scheme: Q13 [2 marks] Knowledge Check

*Question ID: `S05-Q01`*

Award 1 mark per valid feature, up to a maximum of 2: free trade (no tariffs/quotas) between member states; a common external tariff (CET) applied to imports from non-member countries; member states cannot negotiate independent trade deals with non-members (implied by CET); free movement of goods between members. Do not accept: 'free movement of people' (this is a feature of a single/common market). 'A common currency' (currency/monetary union — fundamental category error flagged in examiner reports). 'No tariffs with any country' (too broad — CET applies to non-members).

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Confusing customs union with currency union — the most damaging category error in this topic. A customs union has a CET; a currency union has a shared currency. These are different things.


### Mark Scheme: Q14 [9 marks] Analysis

*Question ID: `S05-Q02`*

Level 1 (1–3 marks): knows a customs union involves free trade between members but analysis is superficial. Level 2 (4–6 marks): correctly identifies trade creation and/or trade diversion; some understanding of welfare implications; may miss the CET role in diversion. Level 3 (7–9 marks): Trade creation chain — removal of internal tariffs → member country imports from lowest-cost partner within the bloc → replaces higher-cost domestic production → efficiency gain → consumer surplus rises → welfare improves. Trade diversion chain — CET on non-members makes previously lower-cost external imports more expensive → consumers switch to higher-cost member-country imports → efficiency loss → net welfare may fall if diversion exceeds creation. Key analytical point: net welfare effect is ambiguous — depends on relative size of trade creation vs diversion, which depends on the height of the CET and the efficiency gap between members and non-members.

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Treating customs union membership as unambiguously welfare-improving. Trade diversion is a welfare loss that partially offsets trade creation. This ambiguity is the source of AO3/AO4 marks.


### Mark Scheme: Q15 [25 marks] Evaluation Essay

*Question ID: `S05-Q03`*

Level 1 (1–5): Basic knowledge; evaluation absent or asserted without support. Level 2 (6–12): Some analysis; evaluation limited to final paragraph; no UK context. Level 3 (13–17): Good analysis; some integrated evaluation; UK context present but thin. Level 4 (18–21): Strong analysis; evaluation integrated; specific UK evidence (TCA, WTO membership, CPTPP). Level 5 (22–25): Sustained evaluation throughout; UK-specific context in every paragraph; conclusion is conditional — 'protectionism may be justified in specific circumstances (national security, anti-dumping, genuine infant industry) but as a general UK policy stance, the retaliation risk from EU trading partners and damage to service sector market access makes broad tariff increases contrary to long-run interests.' Required: definition, UK context, infant industry with conditions, retaliation analysis, WTO constraint, conclusion with conditional judgement.

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Evaluation only in the final paragraph — structurally capped at Level 3 regardless of knowledge quality. Generic conclusion ('on balance, protectionism has advantages and disadvantages') earns no evaluation marks.


## Section 6: Liberation Day 2025 — A Live Case Study

### Mark Scheme: Q16 [4 marks] Data Response

*Question ID: `S05b-Q01`*

Award 1 mark for: identifying that a tariff raises the domestic price of imported goods above the world price (Pw+T > Pw). Award 1 mark for: this reduces consumer surplus — consumers who continue to buy now pay a higher price, and some consumers who would have bought at the world price are priced out of the market. Award 1 mark for: reference to the data — the Yale Budget Lab figure of $1,500 per household, or the tariff rate rising from 2.2% to 27%. Award 1 mark for: specific mechanism — consumers either pay more for the same good (transfer to producers/government) or stop buying (deadweight loss).

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Confusing consumer welfare loss with aggregate GDP loss. Describing the price rise without explaining the consumer surplus mechanism earns maximum 2 marks.


### Mark Scheme: Q17 [9 marks] Analysis

*Question ID: `S05b-Q02`*

AO2 diagram (3 marks): Supply and demand correctly drawn; Pw and Pw+T correctly positioned; Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4 labelled in correct order; areas A (producer gain), B (DWL production), C (government revenue), D (DWL consumption) labelled. Deduct 1 per missing element to minimum 0.

AO3 analysis (6 marks — 2 per group):
- Consumers (2m): CS falls by A+B+C+D. Price rise from $30,000 to $37,500 reduces consumption (Q4 to Q3). Continuing buyers pay more; some priced out — both are welfare losses.
- Producers (2m): PS rises by A. Domestic producers expand output from Q1 to Q2 at the higher domestic price. Gain is redistribution from consumers, not a net efficiency gain.
- Government (2m): Revenue = area C = 0.25 x $30,000 x (Q3-Q2 imports). Less than total CS loss transferred to government because import volume has fallen.

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Drawing the diagram without all six labelled areas. Forgetting that domestic production RISES (Q1 to Q2). Treating government revenue (area C) as a welfare loss — it is a transfer.

> **Hint (for formative use):** Label every area before writing. Area C is tariff revenue to government — transfer, not loss.

**Model answer summary:**

Tariff raises domestic price Pw to Pw+T ($30k to $37.5k), reducing CS by A+B+C+D. Domestic producers gain A as output expands; government gains C in tariff revenue. Areas B and D are deadweight losses transferred to no party. Net welfare loss = B+D. The 8m to 5.5m import fall provides empirical evidence of both the production expansion and the consumption reduction the diagram predicts. The Yale Budget Lab $1,500 figure corroborates the consumer surplus reduction.


### Mark Scheme: Q18 [25 marks] Evaluation Essay

*Question ID: `S05b-Q03`*

Band 4 (21-25): Sustained AO3 chains with well-developed FOR and AGAINST. Conditional AO4 judgement — not a list of arguments but a weighing of them. Specific evidence deployed: Yale Budget Lab, court ruling, retaliation data, MFN problem. Conclusion states which side is stronger and WHY.

Band 3 (16-20): Analysis of both sides with some development. Evaluation present but not consistently conditional. Diagram or data reference present.

Band 2 (11-15): Both sides identified. Analysis underdeveloped — assertion without chains. Limited evidence.

Band 1 (1-10): Mainly description. One-sided or no evaluation.

Knowledge (AO1): Tariff definition; protectionism; infant industry argument; terms of trade.
Application (AO2): Welfare diagram with A-D; 27% rate, $1,500 cost, retaliatory escalation data.
Analysis (AO3): DWL certain under small-country model. Retaliation destroys export markets. Supply chain disruption raises production costs for US firms using imported inputs.
Evaluation (AO4): Infant industry: only justified if capital market failure exists AND government can identify industries with genuine long-run comparative advantage AND credible exit mechanism. Terms of trade: plausible for US (large country) — not transferable to most countries. National security: strong for semiconductors/steel, weak for consumer goods. Court ruling weakens national security framing. Costs observable; benefits speculative. Conditional conclusion required.

> **⚠ Examiner trap:** Writing 'it is both justified and unjustified' without a conditional judgement — Band 2. Must weigh which side is stronger. Confusing economic justification (welfare economics criterion) with political justification (concentrated interests). AO4 marks are only awarded for the welfare economics framing.

**Model answer summary:**

Band 4 answer: defines tariff; identifies $1,500 household DWL cost; argues FOR using infant industry (semiconductor/EV strategy), terms of trade (US is large enough), national security (strategic supply chains); argues AGAINST using observed $1,500 cost, retaliatory damage to US exporters (China 125% on soybeans/Boeing), trade deficit as macroeconomic not trade-policy phenomenon, IEEPA court ruling; concludes conditionally: 'Economically justified only if strategic industry benefits materialise and retaliatory costs are contained. The 2025 evidence makes this unlikely: China's 125% response directly threatens sectors not targeted by the original protection, and the court ruling suggests the legal mechanism was overreaching.'
